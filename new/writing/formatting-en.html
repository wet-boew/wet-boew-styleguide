Acronyms and initialisms

Using initialisms can help to make your documents concise. Initialisms (for which you pronounce each letter) include RRSP, TFSA, and GST/HST. Acronyms (initialisms that you pronounce as words) include RRIF, NAFTA, and GATT.

In general, do not assume that your reader knows the meaning of an initialism. To introduce an initialism into a text or a group of webpages, write the words in full, followed by the initialism in parentheses. If your reader may not read your entire text or group of texts, repeat the introduction in any main section or webpage where you use the initialism. Be consistent, and avoid switching back and forth between an initialism and the words it represents.

You can adjust your use of initialisms to suit particular readers. For instance, if your target audience is CRA employees, you can omit the full name of the organization and simply use the initialism.

Initialisms in titles and headings

In general, do not introduce an initialism in a title or a heading. However, you may want to use an initialism in a title or a heading. In that case, introduce the initialism in the body of the text by writing the words in full, followed by the initialism in parentheses.

Examples

Section 1.2 – Contractual joint venture
In a contractual joint venture (CJV), the terms, obligations, and liabilities of the parties are stated in a document signed by both parties.

Section 2.4 – Contributing to your RRSP
A registered retirement savings plan (RRSP) is designed mainly for saving toward your retirement years.

The style for addresses was developed to emphasize language quality and readability. These differ in some respects from the recommendations published by Canada Post. Please note, however, that Canada Post's addressing guidelines state that "Canada Post's automated equipment can decipher a wide range of addressing styles."

Write out in full all the elements of an address except the postal abbreviations for provinces and territories.

Addresses: Municipality, province or territory, and postal code

Present the municipality, postal abbreviation for the province or the territory, and postal code on the same line. By following this guideline, characters can be optically recognized and mail will not be diverted for manual processing by Canada Post.

Example

Ottawa ON K1A 0L5

Also refer to Geographic names.

Addresses: Capitalization in postal addresses

Capitalize the first letter of every word in an address except prepositions under four letters (like of and for), conjunctions under four letters (like and), and the articles the, a, and an when they are used within a name or title.

Example

Mr. John Doe
Director of Research and Development
4th Floor
Business Building
250 Yonge Street
Toronto ON M5B 2L7

Also capitalize a country name, which should appear alone on the last line of the address block.

Example

Mr. Alberto Garcia-Alvarez
Don Remundo 13, Piso 1
Seville
SPAIN

Addresses: Punctuation

Use punctuation when it is part of a proper name, such as in "St. John's".

Use a comma to separate two elements on the same line.

Example

Mr. John Jones
Director, Marketing Department
ABC Company
Site 6, Compartment 10
Rural Route 3, Station Main
Maillarville AB T0L 1K0

Addresses: Official languages in addresses

For information on official languages in addresses, see item 10.14 in The Canadian Style and item 8.1.4i) of Le guide du rédacteur.

You may translate these types of thoroughfare: rue (Street), avenue (Avenue), and boulevard (Boulevard).

When an address such as 180, avenue de la Cathédrale is translated, capitalize it in accordance with English usage:

Example

Rimouski Tax Services Office
Suite 101
180 De la Cathédrale Avenue
Rimouski QC G5L 5H9

When an ordinal number precedes the word rue, avenue, or boulevard (2e Rue), do not translate it. In accordance with French addressing, include a comma after the building number, superscript part of the numeral (1re, 2e, 5e), and capitalize the first letter of Rue, Avenue, or Boulevard, which is the official name of the thoroughfare.

Example

Mr. Paul Truman
4687, 5e Rue
Montréal QC H1Y 3N5

Addresses: Gender (Ms., Mrs., Miss, or Mr.)

Most CRA systems indicate the gender of the person to whom you write. Use Ms., Mrs., Miss, or Mr. followed by the individual's first name and last name.

When you do not know the gender, use the person's full name.

Example

  • Brooke J. Donovan
  • J. Doe

When the addressee is a woman, and her preference cannot be ascertained, use Ms.

Addresses: Additional guidelines

Order the elements of an address from small to big: floor, building, street, etc.

Example

4th Floor
Connaught Building
555 MacKenzie Avenue
Ottawa ON K1A 0L5

Write ordinal numbers in regular characters (3rd, 7th). Be aware that in Microsoft Word, the superscript effect of ordinal numbers is automatic (3rd, 7th) and should be cancelled under Format, Font, Effects.

Example

340 3rd Avenue

If the official name of the thoroughfare starts with a regular (125 Street) or ordinal number (5th Avenue), do not use a hyphen between the civic number and the number of the thoroughfare.

Example

17 125 Street, 260 5th Avenue

Put the post office box on the line above the municipality.

Example

Post Office Box 12075
St. John's NL A1B 4R5

If there is no unit designator (for example, apartment, suite, unit), use one space before and after the hyphen that separates the unit number (see 100 below) and the civic number (1620).

Example

100 - 1620 Dickson Avenue

If you know it, do not drop the unit designator (for example, apartment, suite, unit) and put it above the civic number and the street name.

Example

Suite 10
9700 Jasper Avenue

Write as one word compass directions consisting of two points (Southwest, Northeast)

Example

645 Murray Avenue Southwest

Bold

Use bold to emphasize words that need to stand out in a sentence. Use bold sparingly, otherwise its affect is diminished. You can also use bold:

  • on links on a web page
  • to indicate steps in a procedure (see the section “Procedures”).
  • indicate GUI elements in software or specific locations on a web site.

Go to Start > Programs > Word and select the Word icon.

Capitalization

Capitalization practices vary widely, and writers often question which words and phrases require caps. The trend in English writing is toward less capitalization, which is known as a down style. We recommend following this style and limiting the use of capital letters.

Capitalize only the following:

  • the first letter of the first word in a sentence or heading;
  • acronyms and initialisms; and
  • proper nouns.

Capitalization: Headings and titles

A heading may introduce a topic within a document or on a webpage, or it may identify a column or row in a table. For a heading, capitalize only the first letter of the first word and any proper nouns.

Example

Headings and page structure

A title is the official name of a document or publication, such as a form, guide, policy, act, regulation, or report. For a title, capitalize the first letter of each word except internal prepositions of fewer than five letters (such as of, for, and with), conjunctions (such as and and or), and the articles the, a, and an.

  • Guide 5013-G, General Income Tax and Benefit Guide for Non-Residents and Deemed Residents of Canada
  • Annual Report to Parliament, 2009–2010
  • Public Affairs and Communications Policy

Capitalization: Proper nouns

A proper noun is the official name of a particular person, place, or thing.

For a proper noun, capitalize the first letter of every word except internal prepositions of fewer than five letters (such as of, for, and from), conjunctions (such as and and or), and the articles the, a, and an.

Examples

  • the Access to Information and Privacy Directorate
  • the Board of Management
  • the Speech from the Throne

A proper noun has one form, singular or plural.

Examples

  • My Account
  • First Nations

Capitalization: Proper noun best practices

Any part of a proper noun is not itself a proper noun when it stands alone or modifies another noun.

Examples

  • Employment Insurance Act, employment insurance, employment insurance premiums
  • Old Age Security Act, old age security, old age security pension
  • Universal Child Care Benefit Act, universal child care benefit, universal child care benefit amount
  • the Access to Information and Privacy Directorate, an access-to-information request

When a noun can have both a singular and a plural form, do not consider it as an official name and do not capitalize it.

Examples

  • business number, business numbers
  • end-state pre-qualification process, end-state pre-qualification processes
  • tax-free savings account, tax-free savings accounts

Capitalization: Institutions and government bodies

Capitalize the proper names of institutions and government bodies

Examples

  • Public Service Commission of Canada
  • Ottawa Carleton District School Board
  • Canadian Medical Association
  • First Baptist Church

Capitalize the proper names of branches, regions, and offices, as well as directorates, divisions, sections, units, and committees.

Examples

  • Winnipeg Tax Services Office
  • Jonquière Tax Centre
  • Legislative Policy and Regulatory Affairs Branch
  • Prairie Region
  • Scientific Research and Experimental Development Directorate
  • Electronic Commerce Compliance Division
  • Process Improvement and Notices Section
  • Small Business Advisory Committee

But when you make a general reference, use lower case.

Examples

  • one of our tax services offices
  • your tax centre
  • our branches and regions
  • each directorate
  • the federal public service

Capitalization: Generic nouns

Capitalize a generic noun when it is used as a short form for a proper noun that has been presented in full and that refers to an organization or group of people.

Examples

  • The Canadian Medical Association has updated its policy on third-party forms. You can find the policy on the Association's website.
  • We work for the Professional Practices Division. The Division has 75 employees.
  • We will consult the Agency Management Committee about this issue at its next meeting. The Committee is scheduled to meet again in September.
  • The Department of Finance Canada is recommending legislative changes. The Department will inform us when the recommendations are ready for review.

But, lower case short forms for the same proper nouns when they are preceded by an indefinite article, a possessive adjective, or a demonstrative pronoun or when they are used as adjectives or are pluralized.

Examples

  • The Canadian Medical Association has updated its policy on third-party forms. An association making such a change should publicize it widely.
  • We work for the Professional Practices Division. This division has 75 employees.
  • We will consult the Agency Management Committee about this issue at its next meeting. We can make a presentation after outstanding committee business has been concluded.
  • The invitation list includes the Department of Finance Canada and the Department of Employment and Social Development Canada. We would like to have representatives from the departments at the meeting.

Lower case short forms for proper nouns naming things, including programs, initiatives, strategies, and frameworks.

Examples

  • The Volunteer Income Tax Program is now in its tenth year. We attribute the program's success to the enthusiasm of our volunteers.
  • The Board of Management Oversight Framework has been in place since the 2003–2004 fiscal year. The framework will be reviewed in the 2011–2012 fiscal year.
  • Take some time to read the Sustainable Development Strategy. The strategy will help us reduce waste and save valuable resources.

Capitalization: Copyrighted names

Capitalize trade names of manufactured products.

Examples

  • Plexiglas
  • Tylenol
  • Xerox

Some industries use capital letters within the name of a product. Capitalize all names of products and terms, including computer language, according to the manufacturer's preference:

Examples

  • Microsoft Word
  • MasterCard
  • BlackBerry

Capitalization: Title of office or rank

Capitalize civil, military, religious, and professional titles, as well as titles of nobility when they precede and form part of a personal name.

Examples

  • Prime Minister Stephen Harper
  • Finance Minister James Flaherty
  • Queen Elizabeth II

But do not capitalize titles in the plural or titles preceded by an indefinite article or possessive adjective

Examples

  • lieutenant-governors of Quebec and Ontario
  • assistant commissioners and other members of the Agency Management Committee
  • a member of Parliament
  • your member of Parliament

Capitalize formal titles that follow a personal name.

Examples

  • Sandra Lavigne, Assistant Commissioner, Public Affairs Branch
  • The Honourable Rob Nicholson, Minister of Justice

Examples

But do not capitalize titles that follow a personal name if they only identify a profession.

  • Jane Tanaka, professor of physics
  • James Hill, publisher

Capitalize a title when it refers to an individual or is used as a substitute for that individual's name

Examples

  • The Minister is scheduled to speak before the OECD on Friday.
  • In the first of a planned series of announcements, the Commissioner unveiled a new direction for communications.

But do not capitalize a title when it refers to a role that may be held by more than one person over a period of time.

Example

To complete the process, the commissioner delivers the annual report to the minister of national revenue.

Capitalization: Parts of a form or publication

Capitalize references to specific parts of a document

Examples

  • Appendix D
  • Chart 5
  • Figure 1
  • Volume 9

Capitalize cross-references to a particular section.

Examples

  • More titles are listed in the Bibliography.
  • See the Appendix for more charts.

Do not capitalize the word section when it is used as part of a law or a set of regulations (see the guideline on Legislation for information on capitalizing references to legislation)

Example

under subsection 12 of section 4 of the Act

Do not capitalize words such as page, box, code, line, note, paragraph, and column.

Examples

  • enter the amount from column 1 on line 421
  • box 14 of the T4 slip
  • code 33 in the "Other information" area

Do not capitalize generic or non-standard references to our forms and publications.

Example

your provincial or territorial tax credits form

Canadian English

Canadian English mixes elements of British and American English. Our pronunciation and spelling tend to be more diverse. We use the Canadian Oxford Dictionary as our principal reference on matters of spelling. You may also want to consult Editing Canadian English, which has a handy set of charts that are a good reference on variations in Canadian spelling.

Set the spell checker on your computer and in your electronic documents for Canadian English. But remember that your spell checker can only tell you if the words you typed are correctly spelled, not if you typed the correct word. For example, a good Canadian English dictionary can help you choose between licence and license, principle and principal, and practice and practise.

Here is a brief list of some commonly accepted Canadian English spellings:

  • cheque not check
  • centre not center
  • traveller not traveler
  • organization not organisation
  • honourable not honorable
  • defence not defense

Commas

Consult The Gregg Reference Manual and 7.13 to 7.21 of The Canadian Style for general guidance on using commas, and make sure to apply the following aspects of CRA comma style.

Commas: Serial comma

Use the A, B, and C (A, B, or C) serial comma structure when you list items in a sentence.

Examples

  • Several federal departments are involved, including Fisheries and Oceans Canada, Health Canada, and Public Works and Government Services Canada.
  • Contact us to get help accessing My Account, NETFILE, Quick Access, or Represent a Client.

Commas: Offsetting commas

Use two commas to set off the title of a form or publication when it appears in the body of a sentence.

Examples

  • Fill out Form TD1, Personal Tax Credit Return, and send it to us.
  • For more information, see Guide RC4027, Doing Business in Canada - GST /HST Information for Non-Residents, or contact the International Tax Services Office.

Use two commas to set off the year when it follows the month and day.

Examples

  • On May 29, 2004, there will be a general meeting for all CRA managers.
  • The June 27, 1997, issue of Time had an excellent article on the new technologies developed throughout the twentieth century.

Omit the commas when only the month and year are given.

Examples

  • We will publish the guide in August 2006 or soon after.
  • She published an article on botanical gardens in the July 1998 issue of Canadian Living.

Contractions

Contractions can give an informal tone to a document, and they are often used in promotional material such as posters and advertisements. You can use not contractions (don't, can't, won't, and isn't) and is contractions (we're, she's, it's). Do not use contractions for had, have, should, and would (you'd, he'd, we've).

Do not use contractions in formal writing such as publications, correspondence, and reports.

Be sure not to confuse its (the possessive form of it) with it's (the contraction of it is).

Dashes and hyphens

Use the long em dash (—) to emphasize a break in a sentence or to present information that is secondary to the main idea (for example, details about or comments on something in the main sentence). Em dashes can replace commas, semicolons, colons, or parentheses in a sentence. Do not include a space before or after an em dash. In Microsoft Word, you can create an em dash with the command Alt 0151.

  • On December 31, 2009, you had residential ties—as defined in the previous section—in more than one province or territory.
  • You must report your world income—income received from all sources inside and outside Canada—but not all of that income may be taxed in Canada.
  • The director general thanked us for our hard work and congratulated us on eliminating the backlog of files—a major accomplishment!

Note: In French texts, the spacing around em dashes differs: insert one space before and one space after an em dash.

Use the shorter en dash (–) in titles of publications that contain two parts or that appear to have a title and subtitle. Insert one space before and one space after the en dash. In Microsoft Word, you can create an en dash with the command Alt 0150.

Examples

  • For information on how to fill out the return, see Guide T4012, T2 Corporation – Income Tax Guide.
  • Please fill out and keep in your organization's books and records Form GST29, Educational Services – Election and Revocation of the Election to Make Certain Supplies Taxable.

To save print space, you can also use an en dash to separate a heading from text that follows it.

Examples

  • Remittance requirement – Under proposed changes, if you exercise your stock options after 2010, your employer will be required to withhold and remit an amount for your taxable stock option benefit.
  • Section 1 – Identification

Use a hyphen (-), without spaces before or after it, in some compound terms and in some words with prefixes (such as co-, pre-, re-, semi-, and non-). In Microsoft Word, you can create a non-breaking hyphen by pressing the Ctrl, Shift, and hyphen (-) keys at the same time.

Examples

  • co-operate
  • pre-retirement
  • re-establish
  • semi-annual
  • non-resident

If you are unsure whether a particular word with a prefix requires a hyphen, consult a Canadian dictionary.

Date and time

Date

Use this alphanumeric form of dating: month-day-year.

Example

September 24, 2009

The number is cardinal (24) and not ordinal (24th).

In the order month-day-year, the day and year are separated by a comma, and the year should be followed by a comma within a sentence. The spaces should be non-breaking. You can create a non-breaking space by pressing, at the same time, the Ctrl and Shift keys and the space bar.

Example

On April 16, 1985, certain additional provisions of the Charter took effect.

Code: April&nbsp;16,&nbsp;1985

The all-numeric form of a date may be appropriate for chronological files and for documents that are presented in both official languages. The format below conforms to the Federal Identity Program Manual (1.2 Message: Identification of programs and services). The year, month, and day are separated by a non-breaking space or non-breaking hyphen.

Example

2009 09 24 or 2009-09-24 (September 24, 2009)

Time

To indicate time, write a number based on a 12-hour clock, followed by the abbreviation a.m. or p.m. When stating an hour without minutes, omit the colon and zeroes after the figure. However, if stating a range of time where one portion includes minutes and the other does not, add the colon and zeroes to state the hour.

Examples

  • The presentation will begin at 1 p.m.
  • The course runs from 8:30 a.m. to 4:00 p.m.

To indicate time within a particular time zone, place a comma after the stated time and write the name of the zone out in full, with an initial capital letter.

Examples

  • The regional directors' teleconference is scheduled for 3 p.m., Eastern time.
  • The Pacific Region is taking the lead on this file. The director will deliver a progress report at our next meeting, which will be on Tuesday, December 18, 2011, at 11:15 a.m., Pacific time.

To indicate duration, where possible, spell out hour and minutes. In tables or in circumstances where abbreviations are necessary, use h for hour and min for minutes.

Examples

  • You have 1 hour and 30 minutes to complete this section of the test.
  • Time to complete: 1 h 30 min

Time: Stating time in French

Different rules apply in French.

Use a 24-hour clock. To indicate the hour, place an h after the number. When needed, write min to record minutes and s to record seconds. A non-breaking space should appear between the number and the h, min, and s.

When not recording seconds, exclude both min and s.

Examples

  • La réunion est prévue pour 13 h 30.
  • Il a couru le marathon en 4 h 23 min 30 s.

For numbers between 1 and 9, you can choose whether to include the zero

Examples

  • Le train est entré en gare à 8 h 09. or Le train est entré en gare à 8 h 9.
  • L'avion a décollé à 18 h 05. or L'avion a décollé à 18 h 5.

If stating an hour without minutes, omit the zeroes:

Examples

  • 8 h not 8 h 00
  • 17 h not 17 h 00

Only use colons to state time in tables (for example, 14:05:32).

Examples

Sometimes it is a good idea to use an example to show the reader how a rule or guideline applies. When you prepare these examples, use names that represent the diversity of ethnic groups in Canada.

FAQs

Questions

  • Personalize the questions—phrase them as though you are the person asking.
    • For example, write: "How do I claim the rebate?" not: "How is the rebate claimed?"
  • Make sure that each question is something people are genuinely likely to ask.
  • Remember that users scan FAQs for key words. Use the terms your readers are likely to look for.
  • Write each question to stand alone. Follow-up questions should appear separately.
  • Ask each question directly, without unnecessary details.
  • Avoid putting questions in quotation marks.

Answers

  • Personalize your answers—speak to the person who asked the question.
    • For example, if the question begins with “I have tuition expenses,” then the answer should begin with: "You can" ...not: "The student must" ...
  • Start the answer with yes or no if you can and then explain any details.
  • Answer only the question asked, and keep the response as succinct as possible. Do not provide extra information.
  • Avoid putting answers in quotation marks.

Forms and publications

When you refer to a form or publication for the first time, always give its descriptor, its letter-number identifier followed by a comma, and its title. Place an offsetting comma or a period after the title, depending on whether the reference appears in the body or at the end of the sentence. Do not include the offsetting comma or the period in italicized or hyperlinked type.

Examples

  • Fill out Form TD1, Personal Tax Credits Return, and send it to us.
  • See Guide T4037, Capital Gains.

How you format the descriptor, letter-number identifier, and title of a form or publication depends on whether your document will appear in print form (including PDF) or on the web. If your document will appear in both print and electronic forms, you must verify that the document's references to forms and publications are properly formatted for each medium.

Forms and publications: Print documents

For a first reference in a print document in Microsoft Word or web PDF, italicize the title of the form or publication.

Examples of a first reference in a print or PDF document:

  • Fill out Form TD1, Personal Tax Credits Return, and send it to us.
  • More information is available in Guide RC4027, Doing Business in Canada – GST/HST Information for Non-Residents.
  • For more information, see Pamphlet P151, Canadian Residents Going Down South.
  • To learn more, get Information Circular IC07-1, Taxpayer Relief Provisions.

For later references within a print or PDF document, use only the descriptor and letter-number identifier and do not restate the title. For example:

  • You can find Form TD1 under “Forms and publications” on our website.
  • Guide RC4027 clarifies how the GST/HST applies to goods imported by non-residents.

Forms and publications: Web documents

For a hyperlinked first reference in a document that will appear on the web, include the descriptor, the letter-number identifier, and the title in the link. Do not italicize the title:

  • Fill out Form TD1, Personal Tax Credits Return, and send it to us.
  • See Guide T4037, Capital Gains.
  • Fill out Schedule 5, Details of Dependant, and attach it to your return.

For later references to the same document or form, use only the descriptor and letter-number identifier (for example, Form TD1 or Guide T4037) and do not restate the title. Do not link the second reference unless it is more than one scroll down the page from the first reference.

For information on how to format references to websites or webpages that start with www., see the guideline on web addresses.

Forms and publications: Lists of publications

When you list forms and publications, present them in the following order (unless you feel it is important to list the most used product first):

  • guides
  • pamphlets
  • forms
  • schedules
  • all other publications

Referring to publications

Use get rather than obtain when advising a reader to find a form or publication—whether online, by telephone, or in person.

  • To permit another person to represent you, get Form T1013, Authorizing or Cancelling a Representative.
  • Get Form RC4288, Request for Taxpayer Relief, and send it and all supporting documents to your tax services office or tax centre.

When referring readers to a publication or form, we recommend using see rather than read or consult.

  • For details, see Form T2201, Disability Tax Credit Certificate.
  • For more information, see Guide RC4135, Home Buyers' Plan (HBP).
  • To find out what to report on line 315, see page 40 of the General Income Tax and Benefit Guide.

However, if you want to emphasize a message about reading, use the verb read.

  • By being prepared, you will help us serve you better. Before contacting us, read the information included in the General Income Tax and Benefit Guide.
  • If you tell us about an amount you failed to report, we may waive these penalties. Read “What is a voluntary disclosure?” on page 11.
  • For more details, read the section in Chapter 2, “Tax on RRSP excess contributions,” in Guide T4040, RRSPs and Other Registered Plans for Retirement.

For more information, go to the terminology decision See, read, consult, get, and obtain.

Geographic names

Use the English name of a province or territory in English texts, but do not translate the names of cities. For example, the cities Montréal, Québec, and Trois-Rivières have only one spelling. In other words, use:

  • Québec for the city (not Quebec City); and
  • Quebec for the province.

For more information, see Chapter 15 of The Canadian Style.

Also see Italics.

Headings - Accessibility guide

Good headings and subheadings help guide readers through your document. At a glance, they can see what type of information you are presenting and where to find the information they need. Since some users skim through a document by navigating its headings, it is important to use them appropriately to convey document structure.

Make sure your headings and subheadings are short, concise, and give a clear idea of the content of the text that follows.

Headings in the form of questions are effective in drawing readers' attention. Question headings can be useful in documents written to promote programs or give information.

  • What is the Canada child tax benefit?
  • Do I qualify?

However, for documents outlining processes and procedures, statement headings may be more appropriate.

  • Building trust to promote compliance
  • Resolving taxpayer disputes and service complaints

Order your headings and subheadings properly, and do not skip heading levels (for example, by going from a level 1 heading directly to a level 3 heading).

Hyphenating compound terms

When using compound adjectives, hyphenate:

  • an adjective and a noun that form a unit to modify another noun:
    • a part-time job; a long-term investment
  • an adjective and participle compound used before or after the noun:
    • a smooth-talking salesman; the salesman was smooth-talking
  • a compound made up of an adjective and a noun with an ed ending:
    • able-bodied; short-handed
  • a compound adjective made up of a preposition and a noun:
    • out-of-province benefits; in-service training
  • a phrase that consists of more than two words, at least one of which is an adverb or a preposition, where the words are used as adjectives:
    • the cost-of-living index; on-the-job training
  • a number, a noun, and an adjective that modify a noun:
    • a 10-storey-high building, a 31-year-old Canadian resident

Do not hyphenate:

  • verb-adverb combinations used as a verb phrase:
    • phased in; make up; follow up
  • proper nouns used as adjectives:
    • a Privy Council decision
  • proper adjectives combined with a simple modifier:
    • a New York chartered bank
  • French or foreign words used as adjectives:
    • a priori reasoning
  • words in quotation marks:
    • a "zero tolerance" approach
  • adverbs that end in ly:
    • richly embroidered; fully employed

Hyphenating: Carry forward/Carry back

When using either of these as a verb (as in to carry forward or to carry back) or as a noun (as in a carryback or a carryforward), do not use a hyphen. However, when using them as compound adjectives, as in a carry-forward amount, use a hyphen.

Common-law/Common law
According to the Canadian Oxford Dictionary, when common-law refers to marriage, you should always write it with a hyphen.

Examples

  • A relation also includes a common-law partner.
  • They are living common-law.

The unhyphenated term common law refers to law derived from custom and judicial precedent rather than statutes. So you can be “living common-law in a common law jurisdiction.”

Hyphenating: Toll free/Toll-free

Do not hyphenate this phrase when it is used as an adverb, but do hyphenate it when it is used as an adjective.

Examples

  • You can call us toll free 24 hours a day.
  • Call our toll-free hotline for more information.

Hyphenating: With prefixes

Use a hyphen if adding a prefix to a word might result in mispronunciation (for example, re-enter instead of reenter) or a change in meaning of the word (for example, re-cover instead of recover, re-form instead of reform, or pre-serve instead of preserve).

Special rules: Use the non-hyphenated online instead of on-line and email instead of e-mail.

Italics

Italics are difficult to read. Do not use italics to highlight or emphasize terms. Use bold instead.

Italics: Forms and publications

Use italics for the proper titles of forms and publications:

Examples

  • Guide RC4070, Guide for Canadian Small Businesses
  • Pamphlet T4131, Canadian Residents Abroad
  • Form T2201, Disability Tax Credit Certificate
  • General Income Tax and Benefit Guide
  • Form T1248 Schedule D, Information About Your Residency Status

Italics: Acts and regulations

The first time you refer to an act or regulations, put the title in italics.

Examples

  • paragraph 14(3)(b) of the Income Tax Act
  • the Income Tax Regulations

If you refer to the same legislation later in the same document and do not refer to other legislation, use the word Act or Regulations as a short form. The word should begin with a capital letter but should not be in italics.

Example

According to the Income Tax Act, the CRA may deem a charitable organization to be exempt from taxes. However, the Act also permits the CRA to revoke any such exemption.

For more information on how to refer to acts and regulations, see the guideline on legislation.

Italics: Foreign words

When possible, avoid using words and phrases from other languages in your documents. If you must include a term from another language, put that term in italics.

Examples

  • Bryan A. Garner states that the adjective inter vivos can be replaced by living, as in the term living trust.
  • You have translated the phrase to take advantage of as prendre avantage de. Most sources suggest that this is an anglicism.

However, do not put the names of businesses or organizations in italics, even if those names are in another language.

Examples

  • Aluminerie Alouette
  • Revenu Québec

Legislation

When you refer to an act for the first time, write the name in full and in italics. Be sure not to italicize the article the before the title of an act.

Examples

  • Income Tax Act
  • Employment Insurance Act
  • Income Tax Regulations
  • The Income Tax Act and the Excise Tax Act contain taxpayer relief provisions.

Writing the name of the act or acts in full and in italics throughout your document ensures that your reader knows what legislation you are referring to.

Short forms

In the same section or chapter of a print document or in the same webpage (maximum two or three screens), you can use a short form after you have referred to an act in full and in italics. However, if you doubt that your readers will remember what act you are talking about, write the name of the act in full and in italics.

Use the word Act as a short form if you refer to one act, such as the Income Tax Act or the Employment Insurance Act. Capitalize the letter A but do not put the word Act in italics. The same format applies to the short form for the Income Tax Regulations.

Examples

  • We use taxpayer in the context of administering and enforcing the Income Tax Act. The term is defined in subsection 248(1) of the Act.
  • The CRA does not publish the Income Tax Regulations. The Regulations are available, however, on the Department of Justice Canada website.

Note

In French, we write in italics the equivalent short form, Loi or Règlement.

Do not introduce the short form (the Act), written between parentheses and preceded by the article the.

Incorrect example

We have identified specific areas of non-compliance with the Income Tax Act (the Act). In accordance with section XX of the Act ...

Correct example

We have identified specific areas of non-compliance with the Income Tax Act. In accordance with section XX of the Act …

Use abbreviations as a short form, such as ITA for Income Tax Act and ETA for Excise Tax Act, if you refer to more than one act in the same document. Remember, however, that your readers may be confused if you use several abbreviations.

Do not italicize or put periods in the abbreviations, such as ITA or I.T.A. for the Income Tax Act and EIA or E.I.A. for Employment Insurance Act. You will find the correct abbreviations in the CRA glossary and in TERMIUM Plus®.

Design guide

Lists - Design guide List group - Design guide Pills - Design guide

If you have a long sentence that contains a number of elements, you can make the information easier for your reader to understand by presenting it in a vertical list. In general, use bullets for the items in lists. Use numbers only when you are providing step-by-step instructions or when you want to rank or show the priority of items.

To create an effective list, do the following:

  • Begin with a lead-in that introduces or applies to all the list items.
  • Include in the lead-in any word or phrase that is common to all the items.
  • Put only one idea in each item.
  • Use hollow bullets for items in a nested list (a list within a list) to distinguish them from the items in the primary list.
  • Punctuate appropriately.

Avoid creating a sentence sandwich where part of your sentence comes before the list and another part comes after.

Although vertical lists can be useful, do not overuse them. If you only have one or two points in a sentence, you generally should not create a vertical list.

Introduce a vertical list with a colon. While this colon will precede every vertical list, there are several ways to punctuate the items. You can:

  • use semicolons after each item (and a period after the final item) when together they complete a sentence begun by the lead-in and the last two are linked by and or or to emphasize the relationship between the items;
  • use periods when each item is a complete sentence and you want it to stand alone; or
  • present without punctuation a simple list of stand-alone items.

How you capitalize items depends on how you punctuate them. Capitalize the first letter of the first word of an item when the item forms a complete sentence and is punctuated with a period. Do not capitalize the lead words in lists where the items are not punctuated or are punctuated with semicolons.

Lists: Parallel structure

All items in a list must be presented in parallel structure. Parallel structure means maintaining the same grammatical pattern for each item, such as beginning each with the same part of speech and using the same verb tense, sentence or phrase structure, voice, and perspective. For example, if the first item in a list uses the imperative (or command) mood, all of the items must use the imperative mood. Or, if your first item begins with a noun, continue listing items beginning with nouns rather than verbs. Similarly, do not include a mix of negatively and positively phrased items.

Example of non-parallel structure

To draft a high quality source document:

  • organize your ideas;
  • only use short sentences and paragraphs;
  • do not use the passive voice; and
  • use the right words;

To draft a high quality source document:

  • organize your ideas;

Links - Accessibility guide

Use hyperlinks to provide access to more detailed information. You can hyperlink:

  • Web addresses for websites or webpages
  • sections of websites or webpages
  • documents
  • descriptive words and phrases

Do not place hyperlinks in quotation marks or bold, and ensure that spaces and punctuation before and after a hyperlink are not included in the hyperlink.

There are two link types:

  • general links to a Web page — attempt to match the link text to the <h1> on the landing page.
  • specific links to a point on a Web page (anchor point) — attempt to match the link text to the natural heading (for example an <h2>) on the landing page.

To avoid confusing the user, only make minor link text adjustments when:

  • a link forms part of a sentence, and maintaining the <h1> text of the landing page (or anchor point) creates an awkward or grammatically incorrect sentence. You can possibly retain the matching text if you put the link at the end of that information chunk and then write:
    • Refer to Content formatting Writing guide - WET style guide, or
    • For further information, refer to: Content formatting Writing guide - WET style guide
  • the <h1> on the landing page includes text that loses context when used on another page for example, an InfoZone link titled Welcome to Manitoba Courts. It is clearer if the link text is shortened to Manitoba Courts, rather than fully matching the <h1> text of the landing page.
  • multiple links on the same page share the same link text, but have different targets. Distinguish the links by adding more specific link text to them.

For information on how to format links to forms or publications, see the guideline on Forms and publications.

Linking descriptive words and phrases

You can also introduce a link in screen text in a general way, using descriptive words or phrases. However, do not include descriptive hyperlinks in documents that will be printed (for example, letters, forms, and PDF documents); rather, provide web addresses in this instance. For more information on how to format web addresses for print documents, see the guideline on web addresses.

For text that will appear on the web, ensure that you include in a descriptive hyperlink only the key term or phrase that identifies the topic.

  • You must meet certain criteria to be eligible for the disability amount.
  • Different rules may apply, depending on whether you contribute to an RRSP.

Names

Use last name, rather than surname or family name. Although last name could potentially lead to confusion for some cultural groups, it is the plainest way to draw this distinction, and it corresponds with first name.

When asking for personal information, do not ask for a full name or full address. Simply ask for the person's name and address.

Notes

Use notes sparingly to emphasize important information or exceptions. Make sure you really want to separate the information you are putting in the note from the rest of your text. If you use too many notes, or notes that contain too much information, they will lose their effectiveness.

Numbers

Spell out numbers for amounts from one through nine. For 10 and above, use the numeric figure. If a sentence has numbers both higher and lower than nine, make them all figures.

Use figures if you are expressing numbers in percentages (for example, 5%) or in millions, billions, or trillions (for example, 7 million).

Spell out a number that begins a sentence (or reword the sentence if you want to use a figure).

Examples

  • Please respond to this report within three days.
  • Eleven people are involved in this case.
  • There were 2 cases of tax avoidance reported in 15 days.
  • Three representatives and 12 accountants responded to our request.

Leave no space between a figure and a dollar sign ($) or a percentage symbol (%). The dollar sign and any currency notation precede the figure. The percentage symbol follows the figure.

Examples

  • We raised $1,000 in our charity bake sale.
  • Based on today's exchange rate, the amount owing is CAN$732.67.
  • Last year, we met our service target 95% of the time.

Large numbers

Wherever possible write large numbers using a combination of figures and words (for example, 14 trillion, 175 million). When using this combination, use a non-breaking space between the figure and the word. You can create a non-breaking space by pressing, at the same time, the Ctrl and Shift keys and the space bar.

If you must write large numbers as figures, use commas rather than spaces to separate each group of three digits.

Examples

  • 14,000,000,000,000
  • 175,000,000

Numbers: Decimals

Use a period to present a number with a decimal.

Examples

  • The supplies budget shows a deficit of $163.32.
  • According to the survey, 85.5% of respondents agreed with our decision.

Numbers: Fractions

For simple fractions in text, write out the fraction in words (for example, three-quarters). For a sentence that contains compound fractions, such as 3 3/4, or a sentence that mixes compound and simple fractions, use only figures.

In general, spell out ordinal numbers that can be expressed in one or two words.

Examples

  • He placed second in the job competition.
  • Help us celebrate our fiftieth year in operation.
  • We cannot be a twenty-first-century organization with twentieth-century technology.

Use numerical figures for ordinal numbers that would require more than two words if spelled out and for address information. Do not, however, use superscript format.

Examples

  • We will meet in the boardroom on the 12th floor at 2 p.m.
  • Yours was the 225th application we received.

For information on how to use numbers to express time, see Date and time.

Numbers: In French

Different rules apply when writing numbers in French. For example, in French texts you should:

  • use a non-breaking space to separate each group of three digits in a large number;
  • use a comma rather than a period to indicate a decimal;
  • include a non-breaking space between a number and a percentage symbol (%);
  • include a non-breaking space between a number and a dollar sign ($);
  • place the dollar sign and any currency notation after the figure; and
  • present ordinal numbers using superscript format.

Examples

  • 158 000 déclarants électroniques
  • 50 % du gain en capital
  • 4,5 millions de dollars
  • 17,29 %
  • 163,32 $
  • 3 225 $US
  • 175e rang

Official languages

Make sure you produce documents for the CRA in accordance with the policies and directives on official languages and the Official Languages Act.

Periods on forms

Follow these guidelines if you draft lines on forms, including returns and schedules.

Use a period when it is a single sentence. In general, a sentence is defined as a subject + verb + object, a subject + verb, or, as on the forms, an imperative verb (add, enter, use, multiply) followed by an object.

Examples

  • Add lines 400 to 440.
  • Enter the amount from line 1 or line 3, whichever is less.
  • Multiply line 18 by line 19.
  • Add the amounts on line 5 of column 1 and column 2, if applicable.

Do not use a period when it is a phrase (incomplete sentence).

Examples

  • Ontario political contributions made in 2008
  • Unused risk capital investment tax credits from previous years
  • Total of amounts your dependant can claim on lines 5804 to 5840 of his or her Form NT428

When a phrase is followed by a sentence, put the sentence between parentheses and start with a lower case letter. Don't add a period.

Examples

  • Universal child care benefit (see the guide)
  • Interest and other investment income (attach Schedule 4)
  • Rate (see the note above)
  • Line 29 minus line 30 (if negative, enter “0”)

When a sentence is followed by a phrase, put the phrase between parentheses and start with a lower case letter. Add a period.

Example

Enter your taxable income (line 260 of your return).

Pronouns and possessive adjectives

Personalize your text

Address your reader directly, using the pronoun you. When you refer to the CRA, use the pronouns we or us, and when you refer to yourself, use I or me.

Incorrect example

Please call the undersigned if you have any questions.

Correct example

Please call me if you have any questions.

Avoid gendered language

Do not use the masculine pronoun he to mean he or she. Wherever possible, rewrite sentences that use gendered pronouns (he or she) or gendered possessive adjectives (his or her) by:

  • personalizing the subject
  • pluralizing the subject
  • eliminating the pronoun or possessive adjective

Incorrect example

An individual is responsible for filing his or her tax return for each year in which he or she owes tax.

Correct example

  • You are responsible for filing your tax return for each year in which you owe tax. (personalizing the subject)
  • Individuals are responsible for filing their tax returns for each year in which they owe tax. (pluralizing the subject)
  • An individual is responsible for filing a tax return for each year in which tax is payable. (eliminating the pronoun and possessive adjective)

Plural pronouns and possessive adjectives with singular subjects

When you don't know whether the subject is a man, a woman, a company, or an entity, you can use
they or them. In English, there is no singular pronoun that signifies he or she or it.

Example

  • We will look at all of the facts related to each particular case to ensure that the taxpayer receives all of the relief they are entitled to.
  • If a registrant fails to remit GST/HST as required, they will be subject to penalties and arrears interest.
  • Choose your tax preparer carefully, and check their references.

If you choose to use they, them, and their with singular subjects, you must use this combination consistently throughout your document.

Quotation marks

We use the North American style (with periods and commas placed before the closing quotation mark).

Examples

  • “Under the Canadian tax system, the government relies on taxpayers to pay what they owe,” said Michel Dorais, Commissioner of the CRA.
  • The data that you enter will prompt one of the following responses: “Registered,” “Not registered,” or “Insufficient information entered.”
  • See line 255, "Northern residents deductions."

Use quotation marks only when you are quoting something. If you want to highlight a term within text, use bold:

Example

A factual resident has to calculate federal income tax.

You can use quotation marks to identify a title or button to which you are not linking:

Examples

Go to My Account for Individuals and select "Log in" if you have an epass.

Do not place linked items in quotation marks.

Special characters

Special characters: Ampersand (&)

The ampersand has traditionally been used as a space-saving character in tables and notes, and in business names like Johnson & Johnson and H&R Block. Except when used as part of an initialism such as SR&ED (scientific research and experimental development), do not use the ampersand as a substitute for and in documents or organizational titles.

Special characters: e.g., i.e.

Do not use e.g. (the abbreviation for exempli gratia, meaning for example). Instead, use for example, followed by a comma.

Do not use i.e. (the abbreviation for id est, meaning that is). Instead, use that is, followed by a comma.

Special characters: No.

Use No. and Nos. as the abbreviations for number and numbers.

Special characters: Percentage (%)

To present percentages, use symbols, not written words. Instead of the word percent, use %.

Special characters: etc.

The Latin term et cetera (etc.) means “and other things,“ “and so on,” or an equivalent expression.

  • Do not use the expression et cetera.
  • We do not recommend using the abbreviation etc. in your written documents. The abbreviation etc. suggests that information provided in the enumeration is incomplete, which may confuse your reader.
  • When you cannot give all the items, introduce an enumeration with including or such as. These words imply that you will only give a few items.
  • Do not use etc. at the end of an enumeration introduced by including, such as, for example, that is, or other equivalent expressions.

However, if you choose to write etc., remember that the abbreviation etc.:

  • is always preceded by at least two elements;
  • is always preceded and followed by a comma, unless it terminates the sentence or is inside parentheses; and
  • is never followed by ellipsis points, but always ends with a period.

Examples

  • For their own protection, employees will not, under any circumstance or at any location, fill out questionnaires, forms, etc.
  • There are many factors that can affect the compliance burden over which we have little influence or control (characteristics of individual taxpayers, tax law, tax policy, etc.).

Special characters: Spelling out characters

When describing characters required either to be entered or their use, write out the character and then include it in parentheses afterwards.

Example

Separate each term with a "," (comma).

Phone numbers

Format both local and long-distance phone numbers that are not toll-free as follows: 514-873-4692.

If collect calls are accepted, exclude the 1- before long-distance numbers that are not toll-free. If the caller dials 1-, he or she will be charged.

Five area codes beginning with the number 8—800, 855, 866, 877, and 888—are toll-free. Do not, however, refer to them as toll-free. That is, do not use the adverb toll free or the compound adjective toll-free before or after these numbers.

Example

If you want to negotiate a suitable payment arrangement, please call 1-866-864-5823.

Make sure telephone numbers are not split over two lines of text.

If you are writing a number that has an extension, include the word extension and a number after the telephone number. Use the short form ext. only if you do not have room for the full word. Remember, also, to put a comma before the word extension.

Example

613-888-8888, extension 5555

Generally, do not introduce telephone numbers with phrases like, "To get information on this topic, call our general enquiries line at 1-800-959-2221." Instead, simply say, "To get information on this topic, call 1-800-959-2221."

However, if you are providing numbers for more than one area, you will have to decide whether to name each area.

Example

Call our automated Tax Information Phone Service (TIPS) at 1-800-267-6999 or our individual income tax enquiries line at 1-800-959-8281.

Web addresses: Punctuation

When including a web address at the end of a sentence, put a period after the address but do not bold the punctuation or include it in the link format.

Example

  • You will find more information about this program on our website at www.cra.gc.ca. You can also call our toll-free enquiries line.
  • You can get more information about the Community Volunteer Income Tax Program at www.cra.gc.ca/volunteer. We offer free training sessions to teach you how to fill out basic income tax and benefit returns.

You can often avoid using punctuation before or after an address by presenting the address in the body of the sentence.

Example

  • For more information, visit our website at www.cra.gc.ca or call your tax services office.
  • Go to www.cra.gc.ca/careers to find out more about career opportunities with the CRA.

Website and webpage references

When referring a reader to a website or a webpage in a guide, form, or other document, keep the following in mind:

  • A website is a collection of webpages. A web address that ends with the primary domain of "gc.ca," such as www.cra.gc.ca and www.netfile.gc.ca, indicates a website. When you refer a reader to a website, use the word visit.
  • A web address that includes a slash and text after the primary domain, such as www.cra.gc.ca/tfsa or www.cra.gc.ca/voluntarydisclosures, indicates a webpage. When you refer a reader to a webpage, use the words go to.

Visiting a website is dynamic—you navigate through the multiple pages the site offers. Going to a webpage is static—you expect to find what you are looking for on the referenced page.

Example

  • For more information, visit www.cra.gc.ca.
  • For details on our e-services, go to www.cra.gc.ca/electronicservices.
