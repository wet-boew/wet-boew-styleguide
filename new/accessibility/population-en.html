Accessibility and the Canadian population Accessibility guide

This page needs to be updated.

The content here represents StatsCan 2001 data, per http://statcan.gc.ca/pub/89-577-x/4065022-eng.htm and needs to have the 2006 data instead, per: http://www.statcan.gc.ca/pub/89-628-x/2007002/4125019-eng.htm

Millions of Canadians benefit from accessible design and coding. In fact:

  • More than 480,000 Canadians aged 15 and over (2.0%) reported a very severe level of disability.
  • 2.3 million or 9.7% of Canadian adults reported having difficulty with everyday activities that require these mobility skills, such as bending down to pick up an object, getting dressed or undressed, or cutting one's food.
  • 7% of the male population either cannot distinguish red from green, or see red and green differently from most people.
  • 0.04% of the female population either cannot distinguish red from green, or see red and green differently from most people.
  • More than 450,000 Canadian adults reported having learning disabilities, representing a proportion of 1.9% of the population.
  • In 2001, nearly 2.5 million or 10.5% of Canadians had difficulty walking, climbing stairs, carrying an object for a short distance, standing in line for 20 minutes or moving about from one room to another.
  • Just over one million Canadian adults (4.4%) reported having a hearing-related disability.
  • In terms of the Canadian population aged 15 and over, 5.0% of Canadians had a mild disability, 3.6% a moderate disability and 3.9% a severe disability.
  • The number of Canadian with vision difficulties was about 600,000 or 2.5% of the population.
  • 12.2% Canadian women have difficulty walking, climbing stairs, carrying an object for a short distance, standing in line for 20 minutes or moving about from one room to another.
  • More than nearly 120,000 Canadian adults aged 15 and over, reported having developmental disability.
  • 360,000 or 1.5% of Canadians aged 15 and over reported a speech-related disability.
  • Limitations related to hearing are more common among men (5.0%) than women (3.8%).
